Part 1 & 2: Project Operations & Vendor Scorecard (Dashboard Context)

Background & Philosophy
Traditional weighted scoring formulas (e.g., 40% Quality, 30% Cost, 30% Schedule) frequently fail to capture the nuanced realities of external engineering partnerships. A firm writing critical regulatory documentation cannot be scored on the same rigid mathematical rubric as a firm performing civil site preparation. Abstracting these metrics into a generic score often hides the raw execution truth from engineering leads.

To address this, the attached dashboard establishes a First Principles, Threshold-Based triage system. Instead of arbitrary scoring, this framework tracks raw metrics and manages by exception.

Strategic Tiering: Not all vendors require the same oversight. Vendors are segmented (Strategic, Preferred, Transactional) based on active SOW volume and overall spend. This dictates the baseline formal review cadence.

Threshold Breaches: We establish objective baseline targets for the three core pillars:

Quality/Regulatory: First-Pass Yield > 80%

Engineering/Technical: On-Time Delivery (OTD) > 90%

Commercial: $0 Scope Creep (assuming standard Fixed-Price SOWs)

Actionable Cadence: Breaching these thresholds dictates the urgency and the agenda of the review. For instance, a missed regulatory milestone immediately escalates a Strategic Partner into a Corrective Action Plan (CAP).

Part 3: Standardized Process & Buy-In Methodology (Concise Version)

Prompt: You are three months into the role and want to implement a standardized process for managing external engineering firms across all active and future projects. Walk us through what that process looks like and how you would get internal and external buy-in?

Processes are easy to write but rarely executed. Internal processes must never be the schedule bottleneck. Vendors are a massive cash draw; we maintain capital efficiency by ensuring we don't risk operational runway with self-inflicted administrative delays. People aren't machines—they execute what works on the ground, not what's dictated in a binder.

1. Buy-In: "Our" Document, Not Mine

For a process to jump from paper to execution, the people executing it must define it. I’d reverse-engineer three historically "excellent" external service projects with the internal team and vendors. We aggregate those shared wins and gaps into a baseline. It can't be my document; it must be ours.

2. Optimizing Friction: The Scars of the Organization

Next, we ruthlessly remove lines. Every unnecessary form is an excuse for the process to be ignored. However, given our nuclear environment, we must explicitly separate administrative friction from regulatory rigor. I will delete internal bureaucratic bottlenecks, but QA/QC traceability and NRC compliance are non-negotiable. We cut administrative fat to give engineers the bandwidth to execute heavy regulatory boilerplate flawlessly.

3. An Illustrative Framework & Organic Adaptation

An intentional framework hits seven beats:

Demand Signal: Who needs it, and what's the budget?

The 'What': Defined SOW, milestones, and communication cadence.

Data Definition: The operational metrics (cost, schedule, yield). Separated from Step 2 so it isn't lost in technical weeds.

Kick-off: Internal/external alignment.

Execution: Run the project, track metrics.

Close Project.

Post-Mortem: Lessons learned and process review.

Step 7 is where we catch organic, in-the-field process adaptations. If we don't document these, we lose critical institutional knowledge during turnover. We write the process down to establish a baseline, then track how it evolves.

1. Achieving Buy-In: "Our" Document, Not Mine

For a process to jump from paper to execution, the people who you need buy-in from have to be the ones who define it.

I wouldn't start by writing a manual. I would identify three historically "excellent" external services projects, gather the internal folks and the vendors who worked on them, and find out exactly what went well and why. We build the aggregate "total" process that incorporates those points of value and identified gaps. Everyone who had a stake in those successes needs to have their hands on this document and their voice heard. It can't be my document; it has to be ours.

2. Optimizing Friction: The Scars of the Organization

Then comes the most important part: ruthlessly removing lines from the process.

Which lines are the most important? What data is absolutely "can't miss"? I don't mean "nice to haves"—I mean must haves. Every form to fill out or non-workflow box to check is just another reason for the process to be ignored.

To be clear: simply having fewer line items does not inherently make a process more efficient, just like having more lines doesn't inherently make it more robust. It is about intentionality. However, given the nuclear space we operate in, we must explicitly separate administrative friction from regulatory rigor. I will ruthlessly delete internal administrative forms, redundant status meetings, and bureaucratic bottleneck tickets. But when it comes to QA/QC traceability, materials documentation, and NRC compliance, that friction is non-negotiable. That is exactly what I mean by "the scars of the organization dictate what must stay." We cut the administrative fat so that the engineering team has the bandwidth to execute the heavy, non-negotiable regulatory boilerplate flawlessly.

3. An Illustrative Framework & Organic Adaptation

I'm not writing a process just to write one. But to illustrate what an efficient, intentional framework looks like in my mind, it generally hits these seven beats:

Demand Signal: Internal need for external service (Who is it for, and what is the budget envelope?).

The 'What': Clearly defined SOW with milestone deliverables, payment structure, and an established communication cadence.

Data Definition: Intentionally separated from Step 2. Establishing the program's trackable metrics (e.g., cost, schedule, yield). Writing the SOW is a technical exercise; defining how we will mathematically measure its success is an operational one.

Kick-off: Internal/external alignment.

Execution: Run the project, track the defined metrics.

Close Project.

Lessons Learned & Process Review.

Step 7 is the most important, and the one that always gets neglected because "we're too busy." In reality, it's the step that lets us continue to move fast safely.

If the process isn't working perfectly, it will change—the team will alter it organically in the field. If we don't catch those changes in Step 7, they just won't be documented. That’s the institutional knowledge that is at the highest risk when turnover happens or new people are onboarded. We write the process down so we have a baseline to track how it evolves.
